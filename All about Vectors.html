Introduction to Vectors

Definition of a Vector:

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. It is usually represented as an arrow where:

  • The length of the arrow represents the magnitude of the vector.
  • The arrowhead represents the direction of the vector.

Difference Between Scalars and Vectors:

Property Scalar Vector
Definition A quantity with only magnitude A quantity with both magnitude and direction
Examples Mass, time, temperature, speed Displacement, velocity, force, acceleration
Mathematical Representation Just a number (e.g., 10 m, 5 s) Represented by an arrow or components (e.g., 5 m east, (3,4) in Cartesian form)

Representation of Vectors:

Vectors can be represented in the following ways:

  • Graphical Representation: A vector is drawn as an arrow from one point to another.
  • Component Form: A vector in a 2D plane can be written as:
    A = Ax i^ + Ay j^
  • In 3D, it extends to:
    A = Ax i^ + Ay j^ + Az k^
    where Ax, Ay, Az are the components along the x, y, and z axes, and i^, j^, k^ are unit vectors.

Operations on Vectors

Now that we understand how vectors are represented and added component-wise, let's move on to vector operations.

a) Addition and Subtraction of Vectors

Vectors can be added using two primary methods:

  • Triangle Law of Vector Addition
  • Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition

Similarly, vector subtraction follows the same process by adding the negative of a vector.

1. Triangle Law of Vector Addition

If two vectors A and B are placed head to tail, their sum R is given by the vector joining the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector.

R = A + B

If two vectors have magnitudes A and B and make an angle Œ∏ between them, then the magnitude of their resultant vector is:

The direction (angle Œ±) of the resultant vector with the first vector A is given by:

2. Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition

If two vectors start from the same point, they form a parallelogram. The diagonal of the parallelogram represents the resultant vector.

The magnitude and direction of the resultant are given by the same formulas as the triangle law.

Vector Subtraction

To subtract a vector B from A, we add the negative of vector B:

The negative vector ‚àíB has the same magnitude as B but points in the opposite direction.
