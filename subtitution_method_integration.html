📘 Integration by Substitution

The substitution method is a technique used in integration where we replace a complex expression with a simpler variable to make the integral easier to evaluate. This method is similar to the chain rule in differentiation.

📝 Steps of the Substitution Method

  • Choose a substitution: Identify a part of the integral that can be replaced with a new variable (𝑡).
  • Differentiate the substitution: Find 𝑑𝑡 in terms of 𝑑𝑥.
  • Replace in the integral: Substitute 𝑡 and 𝑑𝑡 into the integral.
  • Solve the integral: Integrate in terms of 𝑡.
  • Substitute back: Replace 𝑡 with the original function.

1️⃣ Example 1: Basic Substitution

Find:

$$ I = \int (2x+3)^4 \,dx $$

Step 1: Choose a substitution

Let:

$$ t = 2x + 3 $$

Step 2: Differentiate

We differentiate:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}(2x+3) = 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad dt = 2 \,dx \quad \Rightarrow \quad dx = \frac{dt}{2} $$

Step 3: Substitute in the integral

We substitute:

$$ I = \int t^4 \cdot \frac{dt}{2} $$

This simplifies to:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2} \int t^4 \,dt $$

Step 4: Solve the integral

Using the power rule:

$$ \int t^n \,dt = \frac{t^{n+1}}{n+1} + C $$

We get:

$$ I = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{t^5}{5} + C $$

Which simplifies to:

$$ I = \frac{t^5}{10} + C $$

Step 5: Substitute back

We substitute \( t = 2x + 3 \) back into the result:

$$ I = \frac{(2x+3)^5}{10} + C $$

2️⃣ Example 2: Trigonometric Substitution

Find:

$$ I = \int \frac{\sin x - \cos x}{\sin x + \cos x} \, dx $$

Step 1: Choose a substitution

Let:

$$ t = \sin x + \cos x $$

Step 2: Differentiate

We differentiate:

$$ dt = (\cos x - \sin x) \, dx $$

Step 3: Substitute in the integral

Since \( \sin x - \cos x = -(\cos x - \sin x) \), we rewrite the integral as:

$$ I = \int \frac{-dt}{t} $$

Step 4: Solve the integral

Using the standard formula:

$$ \int \frac{du}{u} = \ln |u| + C $$

We get:

$$ I = -\ln |t| + C $$

Step 5: Substitute back

We substitute \( t = \sin x + \cos x \) back into the result:

$$ I = -\ln |\sin x + \cos x| + C $$

🧭 When to Use the Substitution Method?

  • When you see a composite function (e.g., \( (2x+3)^4 \)).
  • When differentiation of a function appears in the numerator (e.g., \( \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \) suggests \( t = x^2 + 1 \)).
  • When trigonometric identities simplify the integral.
